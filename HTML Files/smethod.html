THE SCIENTIFIC METHOD

One of mankinds GREATEST acheivements was developing this technique!

It is a procedure that has taught us almost everything we know.

For the first step, form a question, hopefully one that can be answered! Good questions start with question words: How, What, When, Who, Which, Why, or Where? For example, which cup holds the most water? Which of these four objects do you think will float in water?

Background research can include talking together about what they already know about the question they're asking. Maybe you have a book or have seen a show about the topic. The goal for this step is to engage your scientist brain in some thinking.

A hypothesis is nothing more than a good guess at an answer to the question from Step 1. Ask yourself, "Do you think the red cup or the blue cup will hold more water?" "Do you think the nail will sink or float? Do you think the tin foil boat will sink or float?"

Here comes the part you have been waiting for! Time to formulate and carry out the experiment! Carefully observe everything that happens. Talk out the steps to the experiment as you complete them. "First, let's fill up our pitcher with water. Then, slowly pour the water into the cup."

This step is all about results. What happened in the experiment? Ask yourself, "Did the foil sink or float?" "Which cup held the most water?" At this stage, you should be able to answer the question developed in Step 1.

Scientists share their results! When you make a discovery you should let other people know about it. Talk to your family about the steps used to conduct the experiment and about what you learned.
